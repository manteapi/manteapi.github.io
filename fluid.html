Notes about fluid simulation

Introduction

Fluids have been extensively studied for centuries to understand their mechanics and design mechanical systems. Since the 60's and thanks to the combination of physics, mathematics and computer science, fluid simulation have constantly been improved and can now be performed on a single personal computer. From engineering to special effects in video games and movies, fluid simulation has become ubiquituous. In the following, I keep rough personal notes about numerical models and implementation techniques details related to fluid dynamics that I used. A list of references is available at the end of the page.

Incompressible fluid a.k.a water

The behavior of an incompressible fluid can be describe using the Navier-Stokes equations :

$$ \displaystyle \left\lbrace \begin{array}{l} \displaystyle \int_{\mathcal{V}} \left( \rho \frac{d}{dt}\mathbf{v} + \nabla p - \eta \Delta \mathbf{v} - \rho \mathbf{g} \right)dv = \mathbf{0} \\ \displaystyle \int_{\mathcal{V}} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0 \end{array} \right. $$

where

  • $\mathcal{V}$ is a small volume of the simulation domain $\Omega$.
  • $\rho(t,\mathbf{x})$ is the density at a point $\mathbf{x}$ and at a time $t$.
  • $\mathbf{v}(t,\mathbf{x})$ is the velocity at a point $\mathbf{x}$ and at a time $t$.
  • $p(t,\mathbf{x})$ is the pressure applied on the fluid at a position $\mathbf{x}$ and at a time $t$.
  • $\eta$ is the viscosity of the fluid.
  • $\mathbf{g}$ is the constant gravity.

A more detailed explanation can be found in any books on fluid mechanics. I wrote a small introduction to these equations in my PhD manuscript, it can be downloaded here. Basically, Navier-Stokes equations are the combination of two conservation laws, mass and momentum, a constitutive law which depicts the internal deformation of a fluid due to pressure and an extra incompressibility condition which merges with mass conservation.

Smoothed-Particle Hydrodynamics model

Smoothed-Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) is an interpolation method that can be used to approximate Navier-Stokes equations in a Lagrangian way. It was initially proposed by Monaghan et al. The fluid is discretized into particles which represent small volumes of the whole fluid and each quantity carryied by the particles is interpolated using SPH. I plan to describe some details about the implementation I made of the Implicit Incompressible SPH model (IISPH) proposed by Ihmsen et al.
